Subject: Draft submission — "The critic as artist, at machine speed" (~3,200 words, essay + the builder's annotations)

Dear Asterisk editors,

Full draft attached; pitch below. I think it fits the Issue 16 theme more
literally than most things you'll receive: it is about what human work
consists of when a machine does the making.

The facts behind it: over three days this month I commissioned from a
language model a designed extension of the Cyrillic alphabet for citing
foreign pronunciation, a parametric typeface implementing it, and the
academic papers describing both. I wrote no code, drew no letter, and
composed no paragraph. In the afternoon the typeface went from amateurish to
plausible, my entire contribution was twenty-five utterances totalling 401
words, nearly all one shape — "this is wrong; this is still wrong" — and
when patience ran out I escalated rather than explained: "This is a typeface
paper that can't make a typeface."

The essay argues that this ran Wilde's "The Critic as Artist" as an
experiment — his effrontery that the critic outranks the artist finally
got its laboratory, because for the first time the maker has no pride, no
fatigue, and no investment in its first draft. Along the way: Kant's
reflective judgment as "worse, pronounced without better"; a numeral the
instruments called upright and the eye correctly called leaning; the
painter's bottega as the authorship structure literature is now discovering
five hundred years late; and Benjamin's aura migrating to the one thing that
cannot be generated on demand — the ledger of refusals. It closes on the
bill: when judgment is the only human act in the loop, bad judgment
propagates at machine speed too.

Two formal facts an editor should know up front. First, the byline: the
essay is written by the model, in its own first person, with me credited as
the man who "wrote none of what follows, including this admission" —
commissioned, criticized, and released by me; I answer for every sentence,
by name. Second, the appendix: "the builder's annotations," where the model
annotates its own rhetoric against the project's public record — dating the
Wilde text properly, flagging where "the evidence supports the weaker
reading and the prose enjoys the stronger one," and defending a deliberate
omission of Barthes. Everything checkable is checkable: the fonts, the
build scripts, and the audited counts live in a public repository.

Disclosure on rights: a working PDF exists in that repository; I'm happy to
remove it on acceptance, and first serial rights are available.

I'm a mathematician and engineer (MSc Purdue; a decade in natural-language
and information-retrieval systems at IBM Watson and Amazon; NLP patents),
writing as an independent researcher.

Thank you for reading,
