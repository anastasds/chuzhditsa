Dear Asterisk editors,

Full draft attached; pitch below. I think it fits the Issue 16 theme more
literally than most things you'll receive: it is about what human work
consists of when a machine does the making.

Over a few days in July I directed an AI coding agent through building a
complete designed writing system: an extension of the Cyrillic alphabet for
writing foreign words phonetically, a four-family parametric typeface that
implements it, and the academic papers describing both (one is now on
LingBuzz; two are under journal submission). I contributed no code and no
letterforms. My entire contribution was criticism — several hundred words of
verdicts on rendered artifacts, most of them deliberately withholding the
solution ("why does it look weirdly bold?"), each one archived, coded, and
recomputable from a public record by a shipped audit script.

The essay reads that experiment through Oscar Wilde's "The Critic as Artist":
when construction becomes nearly free, judgment becomes the scarce creative
act — and Wilde's provocation that criticism is the higher form of creation
stops being a paradox and starts being a job description. It engages
Foucault's author-function (and defends a deliberate omission of Barthes),
and it ends with the uncomfortable part: late in the project, my solution-free
discipline broke, and the record shows exactly where and why.

The formal device I'd flag: the essay carries an appendix of "the builder's
annotations," in which the AI that built the artifacts annotates my claims
against the project's public record — agreeing, correcting, and in one case
documenting an omission as deliberate. Every claim in both layers is checkable
against the repository (fonts, build scripts, audit corpus).

Two disclosures. First, the prose was drafted by the model whose work it
describes, under my direction and editorial control — that is the essay's
subject, and I take full responsibility for every sentence. Second, a working
PDF exists in the project's public GitHub repository; I'm happy to remove it
on acceptance, and first serial rights are available.

I'm a mathematician and engineer (MSc Purdue; a decade in natural-language
and information-retrieval systems at IBM Watson and Amazon; NLP patents),
writing as an independent researcher.

Thank you for reading,
