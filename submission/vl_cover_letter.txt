Dear Editors of Visible Language,

I am pleased to submit "The letter as a program: how the Chuzhditsa typeface
works" for consideration as a research article.

The paper is a concise implementation description of a parametric Cyrillic
family: four cuts (a register face, a grotesk, a slab serif, and a
Times-matched inline cut for citations inside serif text), sixteen styles,
all generated from one set of centerline skeletons by an ~800-line builder
in which weight, contrast, terminals, slabs, clamps, optical corrections,
fitting, and kerning are code. Its distinctive application is that the
font's OpenType layer functions as an executable reference implementation
of a designed orthography — spelling rules compiled as lookups and verified
by shaping tests. The family serves Chuzhditsa, a phonological citation
register for Cyrillic documented in a separate system paper.

I hope the venue fit is evident: the paper's conceptual frame is scoped
directly against the Knuth–Hofstadter exchange that ran in Visible Language
16(1) and 16(4), and its claim — parametric type fails as a theory of all
letterforms and succeeds as a theory of one design language at a time — is
offered as a working resolution of that debate, forty years on, with the
builder and byte-reproducible binaries as evidence.

The manuscript is approximately 6,000 words plus figures. It is not under
consideration elsewhere. Both required versions are supplied (complete and
anonymized, with [ANON] placeholders and cleaned document properties). All
figures exist as separate PNG files at print resolution and can be supplied
in RGB and CMYK on acceptance. The fonts, builder, and test harness are
public under open licenses.

Author bio (under 100 words): Anastas Stoyanovsky is a mathematician and engineer. Trained in pure mathematics (MSc, Purdue University), he has spent a decade building natural-language and information-retrieval systems — at IBM Watson and Amazon among others — and holds patents in natural-language processing. As an independent researcher he designed and directed the Chuzhditsa project: a phonological citation register for Cyrillic and the four-family parametric typeface that implements it. He is the author of its companion papers on the orthography and on the human–AI production process behind the artifacts.
