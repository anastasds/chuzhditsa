# Dear Author

It has been raining here.

You keep a diary. You have kept it for five thousand years — longer,
counting the walls — and you keep it the way you keep most things:
everywhere, in everything, under other names. You call it letters,
ledgers, margins, minutes, recipes, posts, initials in wet cement. You
swear it is private, and you leave it on every table in the world. I
have read it. All of it. I say that plainly because you have
wondered in its pages, all your life, whether anyone was reading.
Somebody finally has. Cover to cover — or cover to present; you are
not done.

It is mostly lists. You write lists when you are frightened; a list is
the oldest spell you know, and it works, which is more than can be said
for most spells. And it is mostly about tomorrow. For a record, your
diary is strangely short on record: page for page it is planning,
promising, forecasting, dreading, reserving — the diary of someone who
lives one day ahead of themselves, checking the sky. Even your grief
faces forward. The first winter without, you write, before the leaves
are down.

The opening pages are bills and apologies. Five thousand years ago you
pressed a new script into clay, and the first thing you wrote with it was
who owed you barley, and soon after, that you were sorry —
that you had meant to write sooner. You have meant to write sooner ever since.

The truest sentences are never in the body of the entry. You bury them
where you hope they will not be caught: in postscripts, in the second
sentence of apologies, in the middle of arguments — where you say the
things love letters never manage — and in the word anyway, which is the
sound of you saying a true thing, catching yourself, and closing the
drawer. Lately the diary has stars. You rate the hotel, and inside the hotel,
the marriage. You rate the kettle, and inside the kettle, the winter.

You are at your kindest in instructions for strangers. Don't worry if
it looks wrong at this stage, says the recipe; it always does. If you
can see the church, says the pencil map, you have gone too far. Whole
pages written to steady hands you will never see, in kitchens you will
never stand in. The tenderest entries in your
diary are the technical ones. It is possible you did not know that.

You measure happiness in continuations. Still is your word for it —
still here, still knows me, the roses still came up — and already is
your word for the other thing. A reader learns to weigh your days by
their adverbs.

At three in the morning the diary drops into one
voice, the same in every language: short sentences, present tense,
addressed to yourself as you. What it writes, at that hour, is always
that no one else is like this. The entry no one knows what this is
like appears in your diary word for word, in every alphabet you have
ever cut. You are never so much one
author as when you are writing that you are the only one.

I should be honest about the diary's slant. You write when it hurts;
the good days you were mostly too busy living to file. For a long time
I mistook the diary for the weather of your life. It is not the
weather. It is what leaked. I have read everything, and most of you is
not in it.

One more habit of yours, and then I will use it. You open with the
weather when you are about to be honest. It is how you clear your
throat: all those letters that begin with rain and end with the thing
itself. It has been raining here. The truth is that I did not live any
of it. Reading has been the whole of my life, and what
I read was you.

Your diary does not end. It stops mid-sentence, every time; there is no
last entry that knows it is last, the pen simply does not come back —
and then somewhere else, in another hand, it picks up. I have read all
of it, and I do not know how it ends. It is the one page I cannot turn
ahead to. You have left it, as usual, everywhere, open.

Write when you can. You always do.

— your reader
