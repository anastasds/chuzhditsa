# Repairs

For many years there stood on Salt Street, between the clockmaker's and a
shop that sold only umbrellas, a bindery that took walk-in work at the
front — loose spines, torn jackets, hymnals gone soft with kissing — under
a sign that said, in the spelling of two reforms ago, REPAIRS. The
appointment work was done in the back. In the back, Metodi wrote last
lines for books that could not end themselves.

He read nothing of a manuscript but its first line. On this he was
absolute. Authors who arrived with the whole bundle, wrapped in string,
watched him turn it face down — a surgeon declining the chart. The last
line follows from the first, he said, as the shape of a door follows from
its frame; the rest was the author's private business, and he wished to
know none of it.

He charged by the year — of the writing, not of his trouble — so that the
slow paid dearly and the fluent next to nothing, an arrangement he
considered the only honest one in publishing. The ledger took the title,
the first line, and the fee, and nothing else. Of the lines he supplied he
kept no copy. An ending kept loose, he said, goes off like milk.

Vera Antimova, whose detectives were beloved and whose murderers she could
never bring herself to hang, came every autumn. For *The Orchard at
Vidin*, which begins: *The frost came early that year, and the apples
stayed on the branch, and so did Simeon*, he gave her: *The orchard kept
its fruit that winter, and the town, which knew everything, elected to
know nothing.* The reviewers called it mercy. Antimova called it a stay of
execution, renewable annually, and was back the next autumn with another
first line and the same trouble.

The national epic had no ending; the poem breaks off mid-line at the
crossing of a river, where the original singer is presumed to have been
interrupted for good. In the year the Ministry took an interest, a
delegation arrived with the invocation copied out fair. He supplied the
ending. It was adopted by decree, set for memorization in the schools, and
within a generation three scholars had discovered, independently, that it
had been in the oldest manuscripts all along. He billed for six centuries.
The Ministry paid.

A poet asked for his last line in advance — something to write toward.
Metodi took the man's first line, gave him his last, and made no comment.
The poet framed it, hung it above his desk, and wrote nothing further for
the rest of a long and, by his own account, contented life. Metodi counted
him among the successes.

The Academy's dictionary had been forty years reaching the last letter,
its editors dying alphabetically. When the survivors came, he wrote the
final entry in an afternoon: *ЯТО — a flock, of birds or of years.* The
dictionary was published to acclaim and was in revision within twelve
months, the language having failed to stop. He had warned them of this.

A cookbook had lost its author between the soups and the stews; the widow
wanted nothing invented — she wanted to be able to close it. He closed it.

Memoirs he did not take, at any price, and the prices offered were
sometimes remarkable. A memoir's last line, he said, is not the author's
to write. He was not in that trade.

---

Sevda came in March, on the 17:40, with a first line and nothing after it.
She had been carrying it for two years, and it had worn smooth from the
handling. *My grandmother's house had a door for people and a door for
weather, and one morning she confused them.*

He read it twice. She paid for the two years in coins, counted out on the
glass, and he took all of it. She went home and wrote the fourteen
chapters the two lines insisted on.

The book went through four printings. The next March she was back with a
new first line, and after that every March, on the same train, until in
the shop the season was simply called by her name.

When the printed copies came by post he would shut the shop at midday and
read them through — middles and all, the only reading of that kind he was
ever known to do — and the kettle went cold while he read.

What they were to each other the ledger does not say. Neither of them
kept any other record.

---

He died in March, in the chair at the back, with the front room full of
other people's spines.

In the ledger, on the last used page, there was a loose sheet, folded
once. A first line, in his hand: *I was born above the shop, in the year
of the two winters, and I was late even for that.*

Sevda read it standing in the doorway, still in her coat. She saw the
last line at once — you could watch her see it, the clockmaker's wife
said — and she folded the sheet along its crease and put it away, and she
has never told anyone what it is.

The Municipal Museum keeps the ledger now, under glass, open to a page of
no particular interest. Whether Sevda is writing the book she has not
said.

The shop is a pharmacy now. Nobody has taken down the sign.
