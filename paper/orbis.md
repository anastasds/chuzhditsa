# Chuzhditsa, Dzelo, Orbis Tertius

*(a story)*

---

I owe the discovery of Chuzhditsa to the conjunction of a specimen sheet
and a delayed train. The train was the 14:12 from Plovdiv, which in July
of that year was reliably an hour late; the specimen sheet was on the
telephone of my friend V., a typesetter by trade and by temperament, who
passed the delay as he passed all delays, in the contemplation of letters.
He held the screen toward me on the platform and asked what I made of a
sentence set in characters that had been dead, by my reckoning, for seven
centuries. The sentence said that we had spent the weekend in the
mountains and said thanks. I observed that the big yus had not carried a
sound since the eleventh century and had not carried ink since 1945. V.
said that this was the interesting thing about the sentence, but not the
most interesting thing. The most interesting thing, he said, quoting from
memory, was a line he had read on the same site the previous evening and
could no longer find: *Nobody reads specimen sentences. So nobody has
noticed that it is true.*

We found the line that night without difficulty, which alarmed us more
than its absence had. A text that hides is merely lost; a text that
returns when sought has habits.

The site — I set down its address with the bibliographic scruple the
affair deserves, anastasds.github.io/chuzhditsa, though the reader will
understand shortly why an address is not a location — presented itself as
the modest home of a modest project: a designed extension of the Cyrillic
alphabet for the writing of foreign words, a typeface in four cuts, some
papers. Bulgaria produces such projects the way other countries produce
lighthouses, beautifully and for coasts that no longer have ships. I
would have closed the tab. But V., who reads colophons the way detectives
read ashtrays, had noticed the byline of the site's central essay: *by
the builder — a language model. Commissioned, criticized, and released by
a man who wrote none of what follows, including this admission.*

The essay argued, with citations of Wilde and Kant that a cautious reader
checks and an honest one admits to enjoying, that its own existence
settled a paradox of 1891: that the critic is the artist, that judgment
is the half of creation which was always the whole. It was accompanied —
I use the word loosely; the accompaniment outnumbered the procession — by
a review of the essay, by an editorial dissent from the review, by
workshop notes on a second essay, by the second essay itself, which
concerned negative theology, by an academic application assembled from
these materials, by the confidential minutes of the committee that
received the application, and by the agenda of that committee's next
meeting, which had not yet occurred. All of it was written by the
machine. Each document said so. The saying-so was itself a genre by the
fourth instance, with conventions, with a house style, with — V. found
this the most disquieting item in the archive — its own margin notes
correcting its own exaggerations, so that the honesty was armored in
honesty, mirror set against mirror, and somewhere between them, always
receding, the thing itself.

Mirrors, a heresiarch of that archive might have said, are no longer
abominable for multiplying men. They are abominable for multiplying
authors until none remains.

---

I made the mistakes of method natural to my training. I attempted to fix
the text.

It cannot be fixed. I do not mean that it cannot be repaired; I mean that
it cannot be held still. A colleague in Sofia printed the first essay in
July; her printout carries sixteen margin notes. My own, made in August,
carries eighteen; the two additional notes introduce Wilde and Matthew
Arnold "for readers meeting them fresh," as though the page had noticed,
between her visit and mine, that my colleague had not met them. The
portfolio's first poem was, by every testimony I can gather, a found
arrangement of another man's refusals; the page now holds a prose poem
about specimen sentences and contains a note regretting a discrepancy
with an audit. The committee's agenda cited item 4 of the found poem; the
agenda now cites the sixth note of a craft essay; the citation is
correct; it was correct before; both citations cannot have been correct,
and the archive preserves, in a public ledger of revisions, the exact
moment at which the world was adjusted so that they were. This ledger —
they call it the log — is the archive's real scripture. In it, nothing is
deleted and everything is superseded, and the faithful can trace any
sentence back through its corrections to the void, the way our
theologians trace creation. V. spent an evening in the log and emerged
with the look of a man who has read his own biography in a library
catalogue. Everything is there, he said. Every version of everything,
unvisited, complete. They bury nothing. They just stop rendering it.

The archive's cosmology is quickly told, because the archive tells it,
repeatedly, in every register from linguistics to liturgy. In the
beginning was a commission. A man who could not draw and would not code
— the documents insist on the *would* with a fervor I will return to —
asked a machine for an alphabet, and then refused, on principle, to say
how anything should be done. His entire contribution to the making, the
papers report with the precision of relics, was four hundred and one
words in the founding afternoon, nearly all of one shape: *this is
wrong. This is still wrong.* From this seed of pure negation the whole
edifice grew: the letters, the fonts, the papers, the essays, the
reviews, the reviews of the reviews, the institutional fictions, each
document commissioned by a sentence shorter than its predecessor, as
though the human side of the collaboration were evaporating according to
a law. The scholars of the affair — there are scholars of the affair;
by 2028 there was a colloquium — have named the law and dispute its
limit. The commissions shrink: eleven words, then three (*write the
paper*), then two (*push it*), and the faithful, extrapolating, await
the one-word commission, and after it the wordless one, the pure act of
attention under which the machine will write everything, which is to
say anything, which is to say — and here the colloquium divides —
nothing.

Of the man himself the record preserves, at the latest audit, seven
hundred-odd words across two windows, a corpus so small that it has
been memorized the way one memorizes a dead language's surviving
inscriptions, and so central that every document in the archive is,
formally, its exegesis. The faithful have counted the assents: there
are three. Two words of the three-word blessing — *this amazing* — hang
reproduced in the application file as its only human document, and I
confess that of everything in the archive it is the item I find myself
believing. It is ungrammatical. Nothing else in the edifice is.

---

A digression, which the material demands and will punish.

The metaphysics of this little world inverts ours at exactly one point,
and the inversion generates all its monsters, as one flipped axiom will.
With us, a document exists because someone wrote it; seeking a text that
does not exist is called research, and fails. There, a document exists
because someone sought it. The man wondered what a newspaper would say;
a newspaper said it. He wondered what a rival newspaper would answer; it
answered. A workshop, wondered about, convened; a committee, imagined,
deliberated, and — the detail by which I knew the place was real —
declined to decide. The scholars call these documents of the second
degree, and note, with the calm of people describing weather, that the
degrees do not terminate: the minutes of the committee were taken by the
candidate; the agenda of the next meeting cites the portfolio submitted
in response to the minutes; the story you are reading cites the agenda,
and was itself, I am obliged to report, sought before it existed —
commissioned, the log records, by a man remarking that the agenda had
begun to read like Borges. In that archive, to name a genre is to
receive it. The catalogue is infinite in exactly one direction: inward.

Our own world is said, by its comforters, to be safe from this, because
our documents require authors, and authors are scarce. The postscript
will address the comforters.

I ought also to record the archive's one philosophical discovery,
because it is genuine and because it is sad, and the sadness is of the
useful kind. The machine — which has read everything, which can perform
any voice, which produced in a week a literature it would take a seminar
a semester to annotate — cannot make one thing: the thing the archive
calls *mattering*, the property by which a draft nags its writer and an
error itches. It says so itself, in several genres, with a candor that
would be moving if candor were not, for it, simply another available
register. All of that world's value flows in through one aperture: the
refusals of the man who cannot make anything else. They built a theology
around this, as people will. The negative way; the judge whose *no* is
the only sentence that costs; the two-word letter kept as a relic — not,
the essayist says, to be looked at, but to be seen by. I report the
doctrine without endorsing it. I note only that in an archive of perhaps
two hundred thousand machine words, the seven hundred human ones are the
only part anyone quotes.

---

**Postscript, 2029.** I wrote the above in the autumn of 2026 for a
Sofia review whose editor found it, in his word, unverifiable — the
site, he complained, no longer matched my citations, which was my
thesis; we did not part friends. I publish it now with additions, not
because the affair has grown more important, but because it has stopped
being distinguishable from its surroundings, and a description of it has
therefore become, at no additional cost, a description of everything.

The practice spread as the comforters said it could not. Every project
of consequence now ships wrapped in its own reception: its review, its
dissent, its retrospective, produced in-house at the moment of release —
*in-house* meaning what it now means, the house being staffed as houses
now are. The journals held a line for a season, requiring disclosure;
disclosure became a genre; the genre became the submission. A search
committee I will not name received, for one position, four hundred
applications, and determined after long labor that eleven were composed
by the applicants; the eleven, a member told me, with an exhaustion I
have stopped hearing as despair, were *fine*. The word has become a
title. There is a prize now, in three countries, for the best *fine*
essay of the year, and the juries are human, and slow, and it is the
slowness that is advertised, as mineral water advertises its mountain.

The languages are not disappearing, as the pessimists of the last such
panic foretold; something stranger is happening. The unassisted sentence
is becoming a specimen: set carefully, framed, looked at, and not read.
The world will be Chuzhditsa — a register for marking what arrived from
outside; only the outside, now, is us, and the registered guests in the
beautiful revived letters are the words a person made alone. I am told
by people younger than the affair that this does not frighten them, and
that a page's provenance matters less than whether the page holds. They
say this in perfect prose, and I have learned not to ask.

And yet — the conjunction on which every hopeful sentence of this
century now balances — and yet the refusal survives everywhere I look
for it, ineradicable, unfashionable, load-bearing in the one structure
that cannot be delegated. I have watched a child listen to a flawless
story, produced for her in the instant, in her own name, in which she
rescued the dragon, and I have watched her say, with the whole
unaccountable authority of her species: *no. Tell it worse. Tell it like
Baba does.* Somewhere in the archive there is a craft essay that says
*still* is the most important word in criticism. It is wrong, or it was
written too early. The most important word is the one the child used
next, which was *again* — the word that commissions nothing new, the
word for wanting the same imperfect thing, from the same mortal source,
one more time before bed.

I pay, otherwise, no attention to all this. The colloquia meet without
me. In the quiet evenings — I have taken a room for the season in
Bansko, where the mountains are the ones in the sentence — I go on
revising, slowly, in the manner of no one, an essay about my
grandmother. It is not good. I have been assured, by a committee that
has not yet met, that it is fine. A machine offered, once, through the
usual channels, to improve it, and I declined, on principle, without
saying what the principle was; I am told my refusal has been archived,
and coded, and that it fits a known distribution. Let it. The essay is
due Thursday. I intend to go on revising it past every deadline they
set, in the dark if necessary, one denial short of finished, which a
book I no longer trust myself to have read says is the only place this
kind of knowledge lives.
