# Lost Property

*(a story, in minutes)*

---

## MINUTES OF THE COMMISSION ON LOST PROPERTY

**Station of Gorna Ohrana · meeting the forty-first · present: the
Chair, the Archivist, the Advocate, the Widow Koleva, the
Stationmaster · minutes by the undersigned**

**1.** The Commission met at its appointed hour, the arrival of the
17:40, so that deliberations might be conducted, per tradition, over
the sound of the one event that produces all the Commission's work.
The 17:40 was eleven minutes late. The Commission waited. There was
some discussion of whether a meeting scheduled for the arrival of a
train has begun if the train has not arrived, and the Chair ruled
that the meeting had begun and the world was late, and this was
minuted, the Chair having ruled, in her time on the bench and off
it, on larger worlds than this one.

The undersigned notes that these are his first minutes. The previous
taker of minutes kept this post for thirty-one years, and his pen
was found in the drawer, still good, and is being used. It is a
common practice, the Stationmaster says, to use up what is left.

**2. Item: one umbrella, black, men's, found in the second-class
carriage on the 3rd inst.** The Archivist reported on provenance. The
report was magnificent. The umbrella was traced through its maker's
stamp to a workshop in Ruse, thence to a wholesaler, thence by the
wear on the ferrule — a wear pattern the Archivist described as
"southeast, and stubborn" — to a man who walks with a lean, and by
the mended third rib to a wife or sister who mends with red thread,
economically, being possessed of a large spool of red. At the end of
forty minutes the umbrella had a biography, two probable relations,
a political opinion, and no owner. The Archivist asked that the item
be held pending completion of its history. The Chair asked when a
history completes. The Archivist said he would take the question
under review, and it was minuted that the Archivist has had eleven
items under review for longer than the undersigned has been alive,
and that he visits them, the Stationmaster says, on Sundays.

The Advocate then rose to object that the item was not an umbrella.
An umbrella, he argued, is an instrument for the keeping-off of
rain; the item, having a broken spring, keeps off no rain; it is
therefore, in law, a walking stick with ambitions, and walking
sticks fall under a different schedule. The motion to reschedule
failed for want of a second. The Advocate seconded it himself. The
Chair ruled that a man may not second his own motion, and the
Advocate asked for the ruling in writing, and the Chair wrote it
out, and he read it, and bowed, and said the reasoning was
excellent and would lose on appeal. It is the Advocate's gift that
he can find the flaw in anything, and it is his misfortune that he
has used it. He is known to have disputed his way out of an
inheritance, a partnership, and an engagement, winning each time.

The Widow Koleva, asked for her view, examined the umbrella for
some minutes and returned it to the table. "It is waiting for
someone," she said, "but not anyone here." Her doctrine, which the
Commission has never adopted and never dislodged, is that objects
choose their people, and lost ones are merely between choices. Her
own house is testimony to the doctrine's operation: things choose
the Widow Koleva at a rate the district finds remarkable — pots,
dogs, icons, a beehive, once an entire gate — and she owns, by her
own account, none of them, and lives, of all the Commission,
lightest in the world.

Ruled: held one further quarter. Shelf two.

**3. Item: one jar of buttons, various.** The Stationmaster moved
immediate release to anyone at all, on the grounds of shelf-meters.
The Stationmaster measures the world in shelf-meters and is at
peace. He estimated the jar at a fifth of a shelf-meter, the
umbrella at a tenth, and the Commission's whole inventory of the
unclaimed at eleven shelf-meters, "eleven meters of other people's
carelessness," and he said this looking at the shelf and not at any
member, the Stationmaster being courteous, and the shelf being,
the undersigned has come to understand, where he keeps his feelings.

The Widow Koleva took three buttons, the jar having, she said,
offered. Ruled: released in part. The Advocate asked whether a jar
diminished by three buttons remains the jar found on the 14th, or
becomes a successor jar, and the Chair ruled it the same jar, "as
we are all successor jars," and adjourned item three before the
Advocate could love the question.

**4.** The 17:40 arrived during item three. Nobody descended. The
undersigned notes — and if it is out of order the Chair may strike
it — that his father, the previous taker, was accustomed to watch
the 17:40 come in while writing, and could set down a whole item
without looking at the page, and that the undersigned has been
practicing this and cannot do it. The handwriting turns downhill.

*The Chair, reviewing: let it stand.*

---

**Meeting the forty-second.**

**5. Item: one dog, yellow, medium, found sleeping in the lamp
room.** The dog attended its own hearing, there being no provision
for excluding it. The Archivist traced the dog to no one; dogs, he
reported with disappointment, carry no maker's stamp. The Advocate
argued the dog could not be lost property, property being a thing
and the dog being, as demonstrated by its conduct during the
argument, a person of independent views; he moved to strike the
item. The Widow Koleva said the dog had already chosen, and
declined to say whom. The vote to release the dog to the pound
failed unanimously, which the Chair observed was procedurally
difficult, a motion normally requiring at least its mover's vote,
and the Stationmaster said some motions are brought only so they
can be seen to fail, and this was minuted as the truest thing said
this quarter.

The dog now attends all meetings. It sits by the Stationmaster,
who voted against it twice and feeds it from his coat, the coat
being, the undersigned observes, where the Stationmaster keeps the
feelings that do not fit on the shelf.

**6. Item: one ring of keys, seven keys, no fob.** Held. The
Advocate's observation is minuted for the record, because the
record is where the Commission keeps what it cannot answer: that
the keys are not lost property at all; the keys are found property
in excellent standing; it is the seven doors that are lost, and
the Commission's jurisdiction over lost doors has never been
established. The Chair ruled the keys held "on behalf of the
doors, wherever they stand open," and the Commission sat quiet for
a moment, each member, the undersigned believes, standing before a
different door.

The undersigned's father kept keys on a nail by the kitchen
window. The undersigned has not moved them. This is not procedure
and he sets it down anyway, having observed that the Commission
itself files what it cannot answer under *minuted for the record,*
and that this filing system, whatever the Advocate might say of
its legality, appears to be how the district survives.

*The Chair, reviewing: let it stand.*

---

**Meeting the forty-third.**

**7. Item: one letter, sealed, stamped, unaddressed, found in the
lining of a donated coat.** The Commission's longest matter this
quarter, one hour and forty minutes, the 17:40 arriving unremarked
in the middle of it like a relative during an argument.

The Stationmaster moved to open it, an unaddressed letter being
undeliverable and an unopened one being, at a fifty-year estimate,
a permanent tenth of a shelf-meter. The Archivist opposed: opening
is inspection, inspection is history, and the letter's history
belongs to its sender, who could yet be traced through the coat,
the coat through its buttons, the buttons — the Chair thanked the
Archivist. The Widow Koleva held the letter flat between her two
hands for a long time, like bread, and said it was still warm,
which the undersigned duly minutes and declines to explain, minutes
being a record of what was said and not of what can be borne.

The Advocate then gave the opinion of his career, and the
undersigned, who was engaged that year in trying to understand the
world through the only instrument issued him, sets it down whole. A
letter, said the Advocate, is not finished by writing; it is
finished by arriving. An unaddressed letter is therefore not a
completed thing at all: it is a letter still in the act of being
written, the address being the final sentence, and the Commission
was not in possession of lost property; it was in possession of an
interrupted intention, and there is no schedule for those, and no
shelf either, and every member of this Commission, he said, sitting
down, is in personal possession of several, and knows their weight
in the lining, and has carried them years past the last train that
could have delivered them.

Ruled, on the Chair's motion: the letter to be held unopened, in
the drawer and not on the shelf, "with the other unfinished
sentences." Carried without a vote being taken, the Chair having
looked around the table once, slowly, which among the sitting
members of this Commission is a vote.

**8.** No further items. The undersigned notes that he went home
by the cemetery road, which is not the short way, and read the
stone again, which does not change, and that he begins to
understand the Archivist, who visits his open items on Sundays;
and the Widow, who owns nothing and is owed everything; and the
Stationmaster, who has arranged his grief in meters because meters
can be dusted; and the Advocate, who wins every argument against
himself and brings motions in order to watch them fail. The
undersigned had believed, taking up the pen, that the Commission
was eccentric. The undersigned now believes the Commission is
normal, and that this is worse, and finer.

*The Chair, reviewing: let it stand. All of it.*

---

**Meeting the forty-fourth.**

**9. Item: one pair of reading spectacles, wire, mended at the
bridge with red thread, found in the drawer of the minutes desk.**
Claimed by no one these thirty-one years of service and four
months of silence.

The undersigned brought the item forward himself, which is against
regulation, the taker of minutes having no standing to move items,
and the Chair suspended the regulation before he had finished
asking, citing precedent she declined to name.

The Archivist reported provenance: known. Complete. The first
history, he said, that he has ever been able to close, and he
asked, closing it, that it be minuted that he was sorry to.
The Advocate stated that the spectacles were not lost property,
never having been lost — their location being at all times known —
nor property, the dead not owning things but being survived by
them; he moved that the item be struck from the schedule of the
lost and entered on a schedule the Commission does not have, and
undertook, pro bono, to draft one. The Widow Koleva said the
spectacles had chosen, and had been sitting in the chooser's
drawer for four months, waiting for him to notice, and that they
would suit him if he moved the wire a little, and that red thread
holds better than people say. The Stationmaster said nothing,
measured nothing, and put another stove-length on the fire, the
meeting having gone long, the way he does when the shelf is not
the place for it.

Ruled, the Chair speaking for a Commission that did not require
polling: the item is found. Released to the successor, into
whose keeping the Commission gives also the pen, the drawer, the
nail by the kitchen window, the 17:40, and the practice — which
the Commission notes he has already begun — of setting down,
between the items, the one item that is never on the schedule,
until by the ordinary operation of the record it becomes clear
to him, as it is long clear to this Commission, that nothing
minuted is lost.

These minutes were read and approved unanimously, with one
amendment from the Chair: that the word *father,* wherever it
appears above, be allowed to stand.

Corrections are otherwise invited. None are expected. The 17:40
is expected. It is late, and coming.
