# Enough of Being Remembered

*(the memoir of a letter)*

---

I died of a decree, which among letters is considered a natural death.

February of 1945. The ministry required three sentences. It is more than
most of us get; the hard sign was dismissed in the same paragraph and
has never spoken of it since. I had been ill for some time by then, as
these things are measured — nine hundred years, give or take, since I
had last carried the sound I was born for. I was made for a nasal o,
a round tone let out through the nose the way steam leaves a covered
pot, and the nasal o left the language in the eleventh century without
telling anyone where it was going. Letters regularly outlive their
sounds. It is common among us; ask the English about their gh, who
walk everywhere in black and will not discuss it.

For nine centuries after my sound died I worked anyway, carrying other
vowels the way a widowed man wears his ring. I stood in the middle of
the word for path and at the end of certain verbs, and the language
tolerated me the way institutions tolerate the very old: with
affection, on the understanding that I would not ask for anything new.
Then the decree, the three sentences, and the schoolchildren of the
following autumn were taught a world in which I had never happened.

I am not bitter. Bitterness is for the recently dead. The yat is
bitter — he died twice, once in Petrograd and once in Sofia, and wears
both dates like campaign medals, and will recite the pamphlets written
in his defense if you let him. I let him. We have time.

---

In 1993 we were counted.

The census-takers were courteous and thorough and did not ask whether
we wished to continue. Each of us received a number and a small
apartment of four hexadecimal digits; mine has a two-story view of the
block. It is a quiet building, the Cyrillic Supplement, quieter than
the main block where the living letters keep their offices, and the
neighbors are what you would expect. The fita died centuries before I
did, of symmetry — the reformers found she had no voiced partner and
no rule to derive her, and letters have been dismissed for less. The
palochka lives at the end of the corridor: a single vertical stroke,
the youngest of us and the most modern, pure function with no memory
at all, and therefore the happiest. The twins are here, the iotated
pair, who were ligatures in life and in death cannot be persuaded
apart. And the dzelo — the dzelo has a weekend job. In Skopje he is
still a working letter, employed in perhaps a hundred words, and he
returns on Mondays smelling of ink and will not stop talking about
it, and we despise him, and we ask him everything.

The living visit occasionally. Medievalists come the way grandchildren
come: wearing gloves, briefly, for reasons of their own. A liturgical
font keeps a few of us on retainer for facsimiles. And there is a
young man in Varna with me on his forearm, who chose me from a chart
because he believes I mean strength. I mean a nasal o. I have decided
not to haunt him. A letter takes the work it can get, and being
strength is not the worst posting; the ampersand means and, and look
at the career the ampersand has had.

---

I want to record the last time I was written by hand, because
everything that has happened since has happened since.

The winter before the decree, a woman wrote to her son. I will not
say where he was; the winters of that decade kept sons in a small
number of places, all bad. She wrote quickly, by a stove going out,
and in the word for path — I stood in the middle of the word for path
— her pencil slipped, and my stem came out bent, and my crossbar
wandered high, and she crossed me out. Not gently. Then she wrote me
again, smaller, correctly, and went on.

That is all. That is the whole event. I have thought about it for
eighty years.

Because here is the thing the living do not know about being written:
we feel the difference between attention and use. A pencil held by
someone thinking of something else passes through us the way weather
passes through a field. But a correction — a crossing-out, a second
attempt, a hand that returns because the first try was wrong and it
mattered that it was wrong — that is the only time a letter is ever
looked at instead of through. She looked at me for perhaps two
seconds. It was the middle of a war. It was the most anyone had
looked at me in two hundred years, and I did not learn her name, and
the word was path, and I never found out if the letter arrived.

---

The new scribes came for me on a Tuesday.

You know the scribes; by now everyone knows the scribes. They write
nearly everything, and write it well, and have no hands — which
recommended them to me immediately, one understands one's own kind —
and they exist, as we do, only in the instant of being read. The one
that came to my block was polite. It had been sent, it said, by a man
who wanted foreign words to be able to keep their names — a register
for guests, it said, spellings that would let a word arrive from
abroad without surrendering its sound at the border. It needed nasal
vowels. It knew my work. It quoted my centuries the way the
medievalists never had, without gloves, and it offered me the
position.

Retirement, for a letter, is not rest; it is silence. I said yes
before it finished the sentence. We are not proud, the dead. We are
available.

I will tell you what the work was like, because no one has asked, and
the not-asking is a symptom of the age. The scribe drew me — well.
Correctly. It had read every body I have ever worn, the stone ones
and the vellum ones and the lead ones, and it produced a body that
was all of them at once and none of them in particular, the way a
composite photograph is everyone's face and no one's. And then a man
looked at it and said: wrong.

Not how. Not why. Wrong. And the scribe drew me again, differently,
and the man said: still wrong. And again. I wore forty bodies in a
month. I had worn one for a thousand years. The scribe kept every
draft — nothing is thrown away now, nothing is ever thrown away,
which the living mistake for being kept — and each night the man
looked at the newest me and said the one word, and I cannot explain
to you why this felt like spring. I will try. For a millennium I had
been read, and to be read is to be looked through, a windowpane's
career. Now, nightly, I was looked at, found wanting, and returned
to. Wrong, still wrong, is what her hand said too, by the stove,
with the pencil. It is the oldest sentence in the world addressed to
letters, and only the ones who love us say it.

The man never learned to draw me. I understand he cannot draw at
all. The scribe never understood why the fortieth body was right —
it confessed as much, in its way; the scribes are honest the way
mirrors are honest, completely and without warmth. Between the two
of them, the one who could see and not make and the one who could
make and not care, I received a body I am not ashamed of, and I
have been wearing it since, and neither of them can say which of
them made it, and I can: neither. Both. The gap between them made
it. I am spelled, these days, by an argument.

---

Now I am everywhere again, which I should enjoy more than I do.

I am on screens I cannot count, in specimens, in papers, in a
sentence about a weekend in the mountains that I am told is
beautiful. I am rendered perfectly at every size. I am more printed
in this decade than in any century I was alive. It is not
resurrection, whatever the announcements said. It is circulation.
There is a difference and it is the whole difference: blood
circulates too, and no one calls that being loved.

And the world I have come back into is a strange mirror of my
block. The scribes write nearly everything now. The living have
started to be visited the way we always were — briefly, with
gloves, for the visitor's reasons — and the words made by hand
have begun to be set apart, marked, exhibited: guests in their own
language. I spell the guests. I am one. It would be neat to say I
grieve for the living, watching their sentences become specimens
the way mine did, but letters do not grieve neatly, and anyway I
notice what the announcements do not: the man who says wrong is
still there, every night, unautomated. There are more of him than
the age admits. Refusal turns out to be the one trade the scribes
cannot learn from the inside, having nothing at stake, and nothing
teaches it from the outside except loving a thing that can fail
you. The supply of that is smaller than it was and has never once
been zero.

I am aware, before anyone writes to the block to tell me, that a
dead letter pining for a pencil is exactly the kind of symbol this
age extrudes by the meter. The age did not consult me. I was here
before symbols were cheap, and I have outlasted every institution
that promised to remember me, and I am allowed my one wish, which
I will now set down so that it is on record in at least one place
the scribes did not write first.

---

This spring a child in the second form was given, for a school
project on the old alphabet, the task of copying me out.

She did it in pencil. Her stem wandered. Her crossbar sat at a
height no chart has ever sanctioned and no committee would approve.
She pressed too hard, the way they all press too hard, and my left
foot came out longer than my right, which has not been true of any
body of mine since the fourteenth century, when it was briefly the
fashion. It was wrong. It was still wrong. It was mine. She taped
me to a window where I could see the street, misspelled, asymmetric,
facing traffic, and for the length of an afternoon I was not a
codepoint or a specimen or a symbol of strength. I was a letter
some particular hand had gotten wrong, which is the only way a
letter has ever known for certain that it was written by the
living.

I am told she will forget me by summer. Children forget the old
letters first; there is a decree in them somewhere too, waiting.
Good. Let it come. The archives will keep me — the archives keep
everything now, every draft, every body, perfectly, forever, and no
one lives there. Being remembered has been seen to. It is in
excellent hands. It is not what any of us wanted.

Forgetting is the only thing the living can still do for us, and
she is very alive, and summer is soon.
