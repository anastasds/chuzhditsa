The Grey Bell

a story

In the town of Yavorets it was universally understood that the Angelus was yellow — the deep, considered yellow of quinces ripening on top of a wardrobe — and if it was understood by no one but Evdokia, the town can hardly be blamed, universality having always been the private property of those who pay attention.

She was the last of the Kalofer bell-founders, a family that had poured bronze for eleven generations and buried its dead in aprons, and she had inherited from them a long throat, a contempt for thin walls, and the famous ledger in which her great-great-grandfather had recorded, beside the weight and cost of every bell he cast, its disposition — for it was the settled conviction of the Kalofer house that a bell has a temperament before it has a voice, acquiring the voice as we all do, by being struck. The ledger is in the district museum now, open under glass to a page that reads, in violet ink: For the church at Gorno Ablanovo, four hundred oka of bronze, sixty sheep, one bell, melancholy but honest. Scholars dispute the entry. Scholars dispute everything except their own methods, which is why they are permitted to live in cities.

Of Evdokia herself the essential fact was known to the whole valley by her ninth year, though no one ever succeeded in saying it aloud, the fact being of the kind that dissolves in speech like salt in rain. She heard as other people do not hear — or saw as other people do not see — or, most exactly, she declined to observe the border between the two, the way certain old estates decline to observe a fence the courts have drawn through their orchards. Sounds arrived to her clothed. The Angelus wore its quince-yellow; the little bell of the lower chapel rang a green so young it was nearly rude; her mother's voice, calling her in at dusk, had been all her life the exact dark red of the inside of a violin. She did not consider this remarkable and no one instructed her otherwise, remarkableness being, in Yavorets, a thing that happened to other towns.

Bakhrushin, in the second volume of his Grammar of Instruments — the volume burned by his students, which is how we know it was the important one — writes that every instrument is a translation, and every translation a small treason, and that the only faithful listener is the one who betrays in both directions at once. Evdokia had not read Bakhrushin. She tuned bells. The distinction, if there is one, has never been established.

She tuned them, it must be said, magnificently. By seventeen she had the trade of six mountain districts; by twenty-five the monasteries sent for her the way one sends for a surgeon, at night, with two horses. What the campanological commissions of the capital did with tables and the Viennese comparator — an instrument of legendary precision, never once wrong, having never once been asked anything difficult — she did by standing under the bell in her stocking feet, having it struck, and watching. If the color that fell around her was the bell's own color, the color it had been cast to wear as surely as a man is born to his face, she pronounced it sound. If some other color came down — and a bell can lie in forty ways; there are bells that ring the color of money, and bells that ring the color of their founder's excuses — she would climb into the headstock with her long file, like a jeweler climbing into a watch, and correct the metal until the voice and the color were the same event, which is all that tuning is, though you will not find it put that way in the literature.

The town believed in her without believing a word she said, an arrangement satisfactory to everyone and known locally as respect. They believed her the way one believes a barometer: no theory, results nightly. Asked once — by a magistrate with time on his hands — how she knew the great bell of Trisveti needed a quarter-turn of filing at the sound-bow, she considered the question with the courtesy she reserved for the hopeless and replied that the bell was ringing a violet it had no right to, being a January bell, and January bells are entitled only to the blues. The magistrate wrote intuition in his report, that being the word officials use for competence they have decided not to understand. Brother Pafnutiy of Cherepish, who kept the Synaxarion of Bells — the calendar in which each bell of the valley has its feast day, its miracles, and its vices, entered in red — recorded the same episode differently: This day the bell of Trisveti confessed to the Kalofer daughter, and was shriven with a file, and rang thereafter in its lawful blue. Her fee: bread, wax, silence.

It was the silence she prized. What her senses knew could not be carried across to other people; she had tried, twice, in her girlhood, and both attempts had ended the way embassies to a country without ports must end. The second time — a summer evening, a boy she might otherwise have married — she had explained that his name, spoken aloud, was the pale brown of river shallows over stones, warm where the light got in, and he had laughed, kindly, which was worse than mockery, and asked what she really meant. She spent four years believing she meant nothing: that her colors were a private static, an embroidery her nerves worked at the edge of the true cloth. The belief did not survive her trade. Meaning, she found, is not what survives translation; it is what requires it. But she stopped issuing embassies. She tuned. The bells, at least, had never asked her what she really meant.

The bell of Ravensko monastery was recast the spring after the fire, and blessed in May, and by June the letters had begun.

They were not letters of complaint; the valley did not complain of bells, any more than it complained of weather or of God. They were letters of unease, in the oblique grammar the pious reserve for matters that might yet turn out to be their own fault. The new bell was faultless, the abbot wrote, faultless and yet. The comparator men had come up from the capital with their cases lined in billiard-felt, had measured hum-note and prime, tierce and quint, had pronounced the casting the finest in the district and departed on good roads. And yet. Cattle would not graze within earshot on the ringing days. The beekeepers reported their hives gone glum. The very old of the village came to the liturgy and wept at the wrong moments, at no moment, at moments when nothing was being said or sung that any living person should weep at. The abbot had read of such cases in Ferrandini — De Tintinnabulis, the Bologna edition, the one with the misnumbered psalms — and Ferrandini prescribed either exorcism or retuning, and the abbot, a modern man, preferred to begin with the file.

She stood under the bell on the last morning of June, in her stocking feet, and had it struck.

Grey came down.

Not the grey of any of the greys. Not slate, not dove, not the worked silver of olive leaves, not the noble grey the sea achieves off Kaliakra in November when it has stopped trying to please anyone. This was the grey of afterward. She had seen it once before, as a child, in the year of the other fire — the fire in her own foundry, the one the family did not speak of, the one that took her grandfather and the apprentice and eleven finished bells — and she had assumed, with a child's economy, that the color belonged to that day alone and had been retired with it. It rang down over her now from the faultless bronze like ash settling on a set table, and she stood in it, very still, while the stroke died, and understood that the comparator had not been wrong. The comparator had answered every question it had been asked. The bell was perfectly in tune. It was in tune with something no one had thought to measure.

The explanation, when she found it, was not an explanation; it was a custom. She lodged at the monastery through July, ate their beans, read their registers, filed nothing, and on the feast of the Holy Unmercenaries an old woman of the village, kissing the doorpost of the bell tower as she left the liturgy, mentioned — in the passing way in which the essential is always mentioned — that the whole village had given to the recasting, as was proper. Given? Given, of course. Into the melt. As was proper, as was always done: the spoons of the dead. Whoever had lost someone to the fire had brought a spoon, a buckle, a thimble, a wedding ring too wide now for any living finger, and the founder had folded their metal into the bronze, because a bell that does not carry the dead has nothing to say to the living, and every village of that valley had known as much for a thousand years, and no one had told the comparator, because the comparator had not asked.

The bell rang grey because the bell was ringing the fire. It was not a fault. It was a memory with excellent diction. Sixty spoons were tolling in that bronze, and the cattle knew, and the bees knew, and the very old knew without knowing, weeping at the right moments after all — and of all the instruments ever brought up the mountain, only the woman in stocking feet could stand under the faultless casting and read what it had been given to say.

She did not retune the grey bell. This is the part of the story the capital has never forgiven.

She climbed instead to the smaller bells, the everyday bells, the green matins bell and the two brisk companionable bells that rang the hours, and she spent August among them with her files and her wax, and she tuned them — not to the comparator's tables but to the grey; shifted each voice by hairs and half-hairs until the whole tower agreed with its deepest member the way a household, after long grief, agrees to speak again of ordinary things in slightly lowered voices. She tuned the living to the dead. It is the whole of tradition, said backwards.

The result was measured that autumn by a second commission — the first having declined to return — and denounced in the Proceedings of the Imperial Campanological Society as "a tower deliberately mistuned by a provincial empiric, in defiance of every table," and the paper is correct, and is cited to this day, and has never once been read aloud in Yavorets, where they have their own literature. For when the full peal rang that September, over the pastures and the hives and the graves, the villagers heard — each of them, separately, and none of them able afterward to say it in words that survived the walk home — the sound of being consoled by someone who knows exactly what happened. The cattle grazed. The hives resumed their politics. The very old wept at the right moments, which is to say at all of them, which is to say correctly.

No one in the valley could tell you now which party heard rightly, the woman or the instrument; the question is not asked there, having the shape of a city question. But it is observed that when the grey bell rings, the beekeepers uncover their heads; and the bees, who take no interest whatever in bronze, stand still in the air for the length of the stroke — all of them, every year, like punctuation in a sentence no one alive composed; and whatever it is that stands still in the bees, that, Evdokia said once, filing, not looking up, was the color she had meant all along.

She said nothing else about it. She had learned her economy from bells, who say one thing, wholly, and let it decay for a minute and a half. The Angelus rang quince-yellow over the roofs of Yavorets on the evening she died, as it had every evening of her life; and grey underneath, her ledger notes, in violet ink, in a hand nobody taught her; and after that — the entry is the last in the book, and the museum light falls on it all day, and the scholars dispute it — the two colors together, which have no name, and do not need one, being audible to anybody.
