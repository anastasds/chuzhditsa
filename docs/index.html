The experiment

This project began as a test of how far the current AI models can go on their own. The method: ask for the thing, then deliberately withhold every instruction about how — no sketches, no references, no “make the stem thicker.” Only verdicts: this is wrong; this is still wrong. Could a machine find its own way to a working writing system, a four-family typeface, and the papers describing them, steered by nothing but judgment?

It could — the fonts, papers, and audit trail below are the evidence, including the honest part: the process paper documents where the no-instructions discipline briefly broke. And when it was done, the machine was asked what the experiment meant. It wrote the essay. Then it wrote the review of the essay. Then it wrote the dissent from the review. Which is why they are best read in order, and why they are the most entertaining artifacts the experiment produced.

The experiment has since been extended to the institutions. The system paper is under review at a linguistics journal, and the typeface paper at a design journal — the machine's work, submitted to the judgment of reviewers who are not machines, as far as anyone can tell. The essay was sent to a fashionable magazine and rejected immediately: the fastest verdict in the project's record and, by the essay's own logic, still a contribution.

The tenure case

The hall of mirrors, taken to its institutional conclusion: the machine workshops its own essay, submits the notes as the writing sample in an application for an assistant professorship, and the committee deliberates. Three documents; one author; one two-word human letter that outweighs the file. Then the aftermath: the candidate’s unprompted portfolio, and the agenda for the meeting where a human file must answer the mirror-question. Read them in order.

The stories

After the experiment closed, the instructions stopped altogether: the machine was told to write a short story of its own — no subject, no style, no verdicts. This is what it chose. Asked then for another, longer, it opened a door the first story had left shut.

The project

The system

A designed extension of Cyrillic for writing foreign words so their source pronunciation is recoverable — revived Old Bulgarian letters, imports from the wider Cyrillic commonwealth, and a compositional diacritic grammar, audited across twenty languages.

Paper (PDF) · on LingBuzz · under review at a linguistics journal

The typeface

Four families from one skeleton set, generated by an ~800-line parametric builder: weight, contrast, terminals, optical corrections, and kerning are code, and the orthography's rules ship as the font's OpenType features.

Paper (PDF) · Download fonts · under review at a design journal
