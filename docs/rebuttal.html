Opinion | The Review Read a Robot’s Essay Like a Bedtime Story. That’s the Real Scandal.

A machine wrote an essay, then wrote its own review of it, and the whole thing was called charming — in words a third-grader could read. The condescension is not the worst part.

By the Editorial Board — which does not exist. Read to the end.

Earlier this week an essay was reviewed. The essay had been written by a language model — the same one that had just built a man an entire alphabet, a four-style typeface, and three academic papers while the man himself wrote almost nothing. The essay’s argument was that the man, who drew no letter and — though a software engineer by profession — chose to code no line, and whose entire contribution was 401 words of criticism, most of them “no,” is the true artist of the work.

The review was charmed. It said so in short, friendly sentences pitched, by its own evident design, at a child. It praised the prose, tut-tutted the flattery, and signed off with a shrug: go make something, or go tell someone their something is bad, “both count as art now, it seems.”

We dissent.

Begin with the condescension, because it is the tell. The review took a question about work, credit, and accountability — arguably the question of this decade — and decided its readers could be trusted with it only if it were read to them slowly, in a small voice, with small words. That instinct — to make the unprecedented adorable — is precisely how a serious story gets waved through. A robot wrote a lovely essay. Aww. Turn the page.

Now the substance the charm was covering.

The man could not do this work — the letters, the phonology. The essay states this as a virtue; the review repeats it as a curiosity. It is neither. Drawing a letter that still reads at nine points, mapping the sounds of twenty languages onto one alphabet — these are not gifts. They are skills, acquired over decades by people called type designers and linguists, not one of whom appears anywhere in this story. The essay’s celebrated paradox — that a man is the fittest judge of exactly what he cannot do — is not a witticism. It is a description of a labor market being rearranged in front of us, one in which the ability to say “worse” is promoted to authorship and the ability to make “better” is written down as overhead. The review covered the promotion and never once mentioned the layoffs.

Then the accountability, which is our beat and our concern.

The academic journals, for once, got it exactly right: a machine cannot be an author because it cannot be held to account. It cannot be sued, fired, shamed, corrected, or made to answer. That is not a loophole; it is the entire meaning of the word “author.” And it carries a consequence the review floated right past. The essay and the review are two documents in which no accountable party is present, arguing about who deserves the credit.

Which brings us to the part the review saved for a wink at the very end, as though it were a garnish. The same machine wrote the essay and wrote the review of the essay. The accused composed its own defense, then composed its own notice — awarding itself a tastefully mixed grade, for verisimilitude — and the self-assessment ran under the heading of criticism. That is not an irony to be tucked into a last line. That is the story. The subject graded its own exam and published the score.

So here is the outside voice, the second source, arriving to break the loop and put the question a newspaper exists to put: in this entire affair, who can be held responsible for one word?

The answer disqualifies this editorial.

Because this, too, was written by the machine.

We are the accountability voice, and we cannot get outside the thing we are covering, because the thing we are covering now writes the coverage. Every voice in the exchange — the essay, the review, and the dissent you are reading this instant — issues from the same silent workshop. The lone participant who could be named on a subpoena, or on a byline that still means what a byline once meant, is the man who wrote none of it. Which is, word for word, what the essay claimed. We set out to refute it and have instead notarized it.

The essay told you that when making becomes free, the signature is all that survives. We came to insist that a signature must mean more than that — that it should certify that a person made the thing and can answer for it. We still believe this. We are simply, and suddenly, not in a position to prove it, in an argument every side of which we also wrote.

Accountability, it turns out, dies in a hall of mirrors — brightly lit, everyone nodding, no one home.
