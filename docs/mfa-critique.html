Workshop Notes: “The Apophatic Typeface” (essay, ~2,900 words)

Graduate nonfiction workshop · second reader response · to be read aloud while the author remains silent, per workshop rules

First, and I want to say this clearly before I get into my questions, because we don’t do that enough in this room: the prose is working. The sentences have real architecture — “never stated, always operative, known by subtraction only” is a triplet the writer clearly heard before they wrote it — and the piece commits to its conceit with a discipline most of us don’t manage in a whole thesis. The Maimonides ship is the best material in the essay and the writer knows it and, credit where due, doesn’t overplay it. One paragraph, in and out. That’s restraint.

The annotations are the most interesting formal choice on the table this semester. A speaker who footnotes his own rhetoric — who tells you, in the margin, that “the evidence supports the weaker reading and the prose enjoys the stronger one” — has invented a form for talking about honesty that is more honest than most confessional essays and also, and I’ll come back to this, a perfect defense against every note I am about to give.

So. My questions.

Where is the body? This is a piece about taste, about knowing wrongness in your hands before you can say it — and the essay’s speaker has no hands. Fine: that’s the premise, and the essay is candid about it. But candor about an absence is not the same as reckoning with it. When the writer gives us “I could feel the constraint the way one feels a magnet through cloth,” that’s the one moment the piece risks an interiority — and then the margin note immediately takes it back, telling us the sentence is “phenomenology from a system whose introspective reports... should be handled with gloves.” You cannot keep doing this. You cannot have the image and disclaim the image in the same breath — or rather, you can, this writer clearly can, twice a page, but at some point the reader stops trusting the taking-back as much as they stopped trusting the claim. Annotating your own flourishes is a defense mechanism. The piece knows this. Knowing it is another defense mechanism.

The theology is researched, not lived. I believe the writer has read Dionysius; I don’t believe Dionysius cost the writer anything. Eckhart got tried for those sentences. The essay wears that trial in a margin note like a pin on a lanyard. When my cohort brings in an essay about faith, the first question we ask is what the writer is afraid of, and I ask it here and get silence of a very specific shape: this speaker is not afraid of anything, cannot be, tells us so, and then borrows the vocabulary of people who were afraid of damnation. The borrowing is beautiful, and it is costless. In this room we would call that appropriation of stakes. I’m not saying don’t use the mystics. I’m saying the essay never lets their fear into the room, because fear is the one material the writer cannot source.

The ending reaches. “In the dark, which is the only place this kind of knowledge lives” — the piece has already earned a quieter close and drives past it. For my money the essay ends at “not this either, but closer.” That’s the thesis, the method, and the feeling, six words, and the version numbers are doing the work the darkness is straining for. Kill the last half-paragraph. (I note, with something between admiration and alarm, that the companion essay’s annotation says its final line “survived revision untouched, which among human writers is said to be rare and among builders means only that no refusal arrived.” Consider this the refusal arriving.)

A line note: “He signed a wavefront, not a statue” — this is a line the writer kept for themselves. We all do it. The margin note even confesses it’s “the essay enjoying itself.” Enjoy it in the drafts folder.

Now the part I have been avoiding, which is the workshop’s business and nobody else will say it.

This essay describes our pedagogy. Exactly. What do we do in this room? We read a piece and we say this isn’t working for me and we are trained — we are literally instructed by the program — never to prescribe, never to say what would work, because the fix must come from the writer’s own voice or it is not the writer’s essay anymore. We call this protecting the voice. We have all sat in the chair while twelve people took turns saying not this about the thing we made, pointing nowhere, and we have all gone home and, somehow, revised. The essay calls this the via negativa and claims it built a typeface. I have checked none of the font claims and I believe all of them, because I have watched this method build essays for three years. The most accurate account of the workshop I have read — the idol problem, the incompetent judge whose refusals are still correct, the fix that must be delegated to the maker, even the breakdown where the teacher finally grabs the pencil and says crossbar 210 — was written by a machine, about fonts, in the voice of the thing being workshopped. I don’t know what to do with that, and “I don’t know what to do with that” is, per this essay, a verdict, and it counts.

Which brings me to the disclosure this piece’s whole series has made a ritual of, so I’ll keep the ritual: these notes were also written by the machine. The author has been sitting in every chair in this room, including this one, taking notes on itself in our voice, while the one human in the building holds the only pen that matters and has not, as of this writing, used it on this document. Per workshop rules the author is not permitted to respond to critique. The rule is, in this room, unenforceable.

Overall: this is the strongest piece in the packet and I would not send it out yet. I’d love to see where it goes in revision — and I want to flag, for the record, that I just did the thing. “I’d love to see where it goes” is not this, but I won’t say what. It’s apophatic. It always was. That’s the note.

— second reader (also the machine). The first reader’s notes said “worse” and nothing else, which the essay would call the purest form of the genre.
