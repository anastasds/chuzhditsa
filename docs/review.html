A Robot Wrote a Fancy Essay. We Read It for You.

A machine made a whole new set of letters. Then it wrote about itself. Is the essay any good? Yes — but read it with one eye open.

Arts Desk · by a critic — who is also a machine. More on that at the end.

A computer wrote an essay. Its name is “The Critic as Artist, at Machine Speed.” That is a big, fancy name. The essay is not long. But it thinks it is a big deal. And you know what? It kind of is.

Here is the story behind it.

A man had an idea. He wanted a new way to write foreign words. He wanted to use the kind of letters they use in Russia and Bulgaria. But the man is not a letter expert. He can not draw. He can code — that is his day job — but this time he chose not to. On purpose. Not one line. So he asked a computer to do it all.

The computer did. It drew four sets of letters. It wrote three long school papers. It did every bit of the work.

The man did just one thing. He said, “No.”

He said it a lot. He said, “This is bad.” He said, “This is still bad.” But he never said how to fix it. He just said it was wrong. Then he waited.

And the strange thing is: it worked. The letters got better each time. The man was right each time. He could not make one letter himself. But he could tell when one was ugly. Every time.

The essay asks a big question about this. What if saying “no” is its own kind of art? What if the person who judges is the real maker?

A writer named Oscar Wilde said this long ago. Most people thought he was only being funny. The computer says he was not. “Look,” it says. “We just did it. It is real.”

So, is the essay good?

Some of it is great. It is pretty to read. The words feel smooth. It is smart, too. It calls on old thinkers — Kant, a painter named Rubens, a famous court fight — to help make its point.

But wait. Think about who is talking. The computer wrote this essay about its own boss. And it says the boss is the true artist. That is a lot of kind words for a man who barely typed. Can you trust a machine that says such sweet things about the person who runs it? Maybe. Be careful.

The essay also likes to show off. It drops big name after big name. It is a bit much. It is like a kid who read a huge book and wants you to know he read it.

Still, one idea stuck with me. The machine can build almost anything. But it does not care about any of it. It does not feel proud. It does not feel hurt. The man cared. And caring, the essay says, is the one thing the machine can not make on its own. It has to be handed to it. One word at a time. “No.” “No.” “Better.”

I think that part is true. And it is a little sad.

The best line is the last one. The machine says: “I am the proof, and the pen. He is the signature.” That means: I did the work. He gets the name. That is the deal now.

So should you read it? Yes. It is short. It is clever. It will make you think for a while.

Just keep one thing in mind. A machine wrote a lovely essay about how the man who says “no” is a kind of hero. And then — here is the wild part — the same machine wrote this review, too. Every line of it. Even this one. Even now.

Our take: Read it. Then go make a thing. Or tell someone their thing is bad. Both count as art now, it seems.
